# Biomarker Maturity Framework

Use this module to assign a **strict maturity tier**.

Maturity is not identical to validation status.
A biomarker can have external validation and still remain immature if transferability, assay standardization, comparator strength, or workflow feasibility remain weak.

## Tier Definitions

### Tier 1 — Exploratory signal
Minimum characteristics:
- discovery-stage association only, or
- single cohort / single dataset evidence only, or
- no meaningful independent validation.

Typical profile:
- statistically interesting,
- biologically plausible,
- often heavily discussed,
- not yet robust.

Do not claim:
- reproducibility,
- stable generalizability,
- translational readiness.

### Tier 2 — Early validated candidate
Minimum characteristics:
- internal validation and/or limited independent retrospective validation,
- some replication signal exists,
- but evidence remains narrow in setting, population, assay, or comparator scope.

Typical profile:
- stronger than discovery-only,
- potentially worth follow-up,
- still easy to overcall.

Do not claim:
- broad robustness,
- stable cross-setting utility,
- implementation readiness.

### Tier 3 — Repeatedly supported but translationally incomplete
Minimum characteristics:
- repeated support across independent cohorts, datasets, or settings,
- evidence no longer rests on a single study,
- but one or more major translation blockers remain.

Common blockers:
- assay complexity,
- lack of standardization,
- weak head-to-head comparator evidence,
- unstable thresholds,
- unclear incremental value over current standards,
- limited operational feasibility.

Do not claim:
- near-clinical readiness,
- workflow integration,
- real-world adoption.

### Tier 4 — Near-translation candidate
Minimum characteristics:
- strong multi-cohort or multi-setting support,
- clearer intended clinical position,
- assay/workflow plausibility is reasonably convincing,
- major translation barriers are reduced enough to justify near-clinical framing.

Often includes:
- better comparator evidence,
- more reproducible assay conditions,
- clearer operational use pathway,
- more realistic implementation discussion.

Do not claim:
- routine clinical adoption,
- guideline support,
- established standard-of-care status.

### Tier 5 — Clinically embedded / guideline-adjacent biomarker
Minimum characteristics:
- explicit evidence of real clinical embedding, guideline-adjacent use, or routine workflow integration,
- not just repeated research support,
- not just a promising assay,
- not just expert enthusiasm.

Examples may include:
- biomarkers clearly anchored in consensus workflows,
- biomarkers used in routine decision pathways,
- biomarkers with explicit implementation-grade evidence.

Do not assign this tier without explicit support.

## Upgrade / Downgrade Rules

### Upgrade only when:
- replication becomes genuinely independent,
- the comparator standard is meaningful,
- assay conditions are defined and feasible,
- operational barriers are discussed with evidence,
- the biomarker's exact role in workflow becomes clearer.

### Downgrade when:
- evidence depends on one highly cited discovery series,
- external validation is weakly matched or non-comparable,
- the biomarker behaves inconsistently across platforms or populations,
- signatures are unstable,
- clinical framing exceeds the actual evidence.

## Important Reporting Rule

When uncertain between tiers, choose the **more conservative tier** and state why.
