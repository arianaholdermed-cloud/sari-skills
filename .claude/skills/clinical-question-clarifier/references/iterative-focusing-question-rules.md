# Iterative Focusing Question Rules

Use guided follow-up questions when the user needs help narrowing a vague or over-broad idea.

## When to Trigger Guided Focusing
- the user gives a theme rather than a real question
- two or more core elements are missing
- multiple question types are blended together
- the user explicitly asks for step-by-step refinement
- the first-pass formulation would require too many unstated assumptions

## Question Prioritization Rule
Ask the questions that reduce ambiguity the most, in roughly this order:
1. what kind of question is this really: treatment, prognosis, diagnosis, mechanism, risk, or research planning?
2. what population, disease stage, subtype, or context matters most?
3. what intervention, exposure, biomarker, mechanism, or comparison is central?
4. what outcome or intended use-case matters most?
5. what boundary would make this manageable right now?

## Keep It Short
Ask 2–5 concise, high-yield questions, not a full intake form.

## After Each Reply
- restate the updated understanding briefly
- identify what ambiguity remains
- either ask the next highest-yield question or move to formal clarification

## Stop Rule
Stop asking questions once the problem is bounded enough to produce:
- a clear dominant question type
- a usable structured breakdown
- a searchable version
- a research-ready version

## Failure Modes
- asking too many low-value questions
- asking questions out of logical order
- keeping the dialogue open-ended when the topic is already sufficiently focused
- moving to final framing without resolving the ambiguity that most affects searchability or researchability
