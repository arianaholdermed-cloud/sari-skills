# Scientific Research Reporting Guidelines

## Overview

Reporting guidelines are evidence-based recommendations that specify what information should be included when reporting a specific type of research. They provide checklists and flow diagrams to ensure complete, accurate, and transparent reporting, which is essential for readers to assess the validity of a study and for other researchers to replicate the work.

The EQUATOR Network (Enhancing the QUAlity and Transparency Of health Research) maintains a comprehensive library of reporting guidelines. Using the appropriate reporting guideline can improve manuscript quality and increase the likelihood of publication acceptance.

## Why Use Reporting Guidelines?

### Benefits

**For Authors:**
- Ensures no important content is forgotten
- Increases acceptance rates
- Improves manuscript organization
- Reduces reviewer requests for supplemental information

**For Readers and Reviewers:**
- Enables critical appraisal of study validity
- Facilitates systematic reviews and meta-analyses
- Improves understanding of practical implementation

**For Science:**
- Enhances reproducibility
- Reduces research waste
- Increases transparency
- Facilitates better evidence synthesis

### When to Use

- **During Research Design**: Many guidelines include protocol versions (e.g., SPIRIT for trials)
- **During Manuscript Drafting**: Use checklists to ensure all items are covered
- **Before Submission**: Verify compliance and often submit the checklist along with the manuscript
- **Required by Many Journals**: Reporting guideline checklists are often required as part of the submission

## Major Reporting Guidelines by Research Type

### CONSORT - Randomized Controlled Trials

**Full Name**: Consolidated Standards of Reporting Trials

**When to Use**: Any randomized controlled trial (RCT), including pilot and feasibility trials

**Latest Version**: CONSORT 2010 (updated statement)

**Key Components:**
- **Checklist**: 25 items covering Title, Abstract, Introduction, Methods, Results, Discussion
- **Flow Diagram**: Participant flow from enrollment, allocation, follow-up, to analysis

**Major Checklist Items:**
1. Title identifying the study as a randomized trial
2. Structured abstract
3. Scientific background and explanation of rationale
4. Specific objectives and hypotheses
5. Description of trial design (parallel, crossover, factorial, etc.)
6. Inclusion criteria for participants
7. Settings and locations where data were collected
8. Interventions described with sufficient detail to allow replication
9. Primary and secondary outcome definitions
10. Sample size determination and power calculation
11. Randomization sequence generation
12. Allocation concealment mechanism
13. Implementation of blinding
14. Statistical methods
15. Participant flow with reasons for loss to follow-up
16. Recruitment dates and follow-up periods
17. Table of baseline characteristics
18. Analysis results for each outcome
19. Harms and adverse events
20. Trial limitations
21. Generalizability
22. Interpretation consistent with results
23. Trial registration number
24. Full protocol access
25. Sources of funding

**Extensions for Specific Designs:**
- CONSORT for Cluster Randomized Trials
- CONSORT for Non-inferiority and Equivalence Trials
- CONSORT for Pragmatic Trials
- CONSORT for Crossover Trials
- CONSORT for N-of-1 Trials
- CONSORT for Stepped Wedge Designs

**Access**: http://www.consort-statement.org/

### STROBE - Observational Studies

**Full Name**: Strengthening the Reporting of Observational Studies in Epidemiology

**When to Use**: Cohort studies, case-control studies, and cross-sectional studies

**Latest Version**: STROBE 2007 (widely adopted standard)

**Major Study Designs Covered:**
- **Cohort**: Follows exposed and unexposed groups forward in time
- **Case-control**: Compares exposure history of cases and controls
- **Cross-sectional**: Measures exposure and outcome at the same time

**Major Checklist Items (22 items):**
1. Title and abstract indicating study design
2. Background and rationale
3. Objectives
4. Study design and rationale
5. Setting, locations, and dates
6. Inclusion criteria and selection methods
7. Variables clearly defined (outcomes, exposures, confounders)
8. Data sources and measurement methods
9. Bias management strategies
10. Study size justification
11. Handling of quantitative variables
12. Statistical methods including confounding and interactions
13. Sensitivity analyses
14. Participant flow with reasons for non-participation
15. Descriptive data including follow-up time
16. Outcome data
17. Main results with unadjusted and adjusted estimates
18. Other analyses (subgroups, sensitivity analyses)
19. Summary of key results
20. Limitations with discussion of potential bias
21. Interpretation and generalizability
22. Funding sources and role

**Extensions:**
- STROBE-ME (Molecular Epidemiology)
- RECORD (Routinely-collected Health Data)
- STROBE-RDS (Respondent-Driven Sampling)

**Access**: https://www.strobe-statement.org/

### PRISMA - Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses

**Full Name**: Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses

**When to Use**: Systematic reviews with or without meta-analyses

**Latest Version**: PRISMA 2020 (major update)

**Key Components:**
- **Checklist**: 27 items covering all sections
- **Flow Diagram**: Study selection process

**Major Sections:**
1. **Title**: Identify as systematic review/meta-analysis
2. **Abstract**: Structured summary
3. **Introduction**: Rationale and objectives
4. **Methods**:
   - Eligibility criteria
   - Information sources (databases, dates)
   - Search strategy (full strategy for at least one database)
   - Selection process
   - Data collection process
   - Data items extracted
   - Risk of bias assessment
   - Effect measures
   - Synthesis methods
   - Reporting bias assessment
   - Certainty of evidence assessment (e.g., GRADE)
5. **Results**:
   - Study selection flow diagram
   - Study characteristics
   - Bias assessment results
   - Synthesis results (if applicable)
   - Reporting bias
   - Certainty of evidence
6. **Discussion**:
   - Limitations
   - Interpretation
   - Implications

**Extensions:**
- PRISMA for Abstracts
- PRISMA for Protocols (PRISMA-P)
- PRISMA for Network Meta-Analyses
- PRISMA for Scoping Reviews (PRISMA-ScR)
- PRISMA for Individual Patient Data
- PRISMA for Diagnostic Test Accuracy
- PRISMA for Equity-focused Reviews

**Access**: http://www.prisma-statement.org/

### SPIRIT - Clinical Trial Protocols

**Full Name**: Standard Protocol Items: Recommendations for Interventional Trials

**When to Use**: Protocols for randomized trials and other planned intervention studies

**Latest Version**: SPIRIT 2013

**Purpose**: To ensure trial protocols contain a complete description before the trial begins

**Major Checklist Items (33 items):**
- Administrative information (Title, Trial registration, Funding)
- Introduction (Background, Rationale, Objectives)
- Methods: Trial Design
  - Study setting
  - Eligibility criteria
  - Intervention details
  - Outcomes (primary and secondary)
  - Participant timeline
  - Sample size calculation
  - Recruitment strategy
  - Allocation and randomization
  - Blinding
  - Data collection methods
  - Data management
  - Statistical methods
  - Monitoring (Data Monitoring Committee)
  - Harms reporting
  - Auditing
- Ethics and Dissemination
  - Ethical approval
  - Informed consent procedures
  - Confidentiality
  - Dissemination plans

**Access**: https://www.spirit-statement.org/

### STARD - Diagnostic Accuracy Studies

**Full Name**: Standards for Reporting of Diagnostic Accuracy Studies

**When to Use**: Studies evaluating the accuracy of a diagnostic test

**Latest Version**: STARD 2015

**Major Checklist Items (30 items):**
1. Study design identification
2. Background information and objectives
3. Study design description
4. Participant selection criteria and recruitment
5. Data collection methods
6. Index test description and execution
7. Reference standard description
8. Rationale for choosing reference standard
9. Test result definitions and cut-offs
10. Participant flow with timing
11. Baseline demographic and clinical characteristics
12. Cross-tabulation of index test results by reference standard
13. Diagnostic accuracy estimates with confidence intervals
14. Handling of indeterminate results
15. Adverse events from testing

**Flow Diagram**: Showing the flow of participants and test results

**Access**: https://www.equator-network.org/reporting-guidelines/stard/

### TRIPOD - Prediction Model Studies

**Full Name**: Transparent Reporting of a multivariable prediction model for Individual Prognosis Or Diagnosis

**When to Use**: Studies developing, validating, or updating prediction models

**Latest Version**: TRIPOD 2015

**Study Types:**
- Model development only
- Model development plus validation
- External validation of an existing model
- Model updating

**Major Checklist Items (22 items):**
1. Title identifying the study as a prediction model study
2. Abstract summarizing key elements
3. Background and objectives
4. Data sources and participants
5. Outcome definition
6. Predictors (candidate and selected)
7. Sample size justification
8. Handling of missing data
9. Model building procedures
10. Model specification (equation or algorithm)
11. Model performance measures
12. Risk grouping if used
13. Participant flow diagram
14. Model development results
15. Model performance
16. Model updating if applicable

**Access**: https://www.tripod-statement.org/

### ARRIVE - Animal Research

**Full Name**: Animal Research: Reporting of In Vivo Experiments

**When to Use**: All in vivo animal research

**Latest Version**: ARRIVE 2.0 (updated 2020)

**Two Sets of Items:**

**ARRIVE Essential 10** (Minimum requirement):
1. Study design
2. Sample size calculation
3. Inclusion and exclusion criteria
4. Randomization
5. Blinding
6. Outcome measures
7. Statistical methods
8. Experimental animals (species, strain, sex, age)
9. Experimental procedures
10. Results and interpretation

**ARRIVE Recommended Set** (Additional items for complete reporting):
- Abstract, Background, Objectives
- Ethical statement
- Housing and husbandry
- Animal care and monitoring
- Interpretation and generalizability
- Protocol registration
- Data access

**Access**: https://arriveguidelines.org/

### CARE - Case Reports

**Full Name**: CAse REport Guidelines

**When to Use**: Case reports and case series

**Latest Version**: CARE 2013

**Major Checklist Items (13 items):**
1. Title containing "case report"
2. Abstract summarizing the case
3. Introduction with case background
4. Patient information (demographics, main concerns)
5. Clinical findings
6. Timeline of events
7. Diagnostic assessment
8. Therapeutic intervention
9. Follow-up and outcomes
10. Discussion of strengths and limitations
11. Patient perspective
12. Informed consent

**Access**: https://www.care-statement.org/

### SQUIRE - Quality Improvement Research

**Full Name**: Standards for QUality Improvement Reporting Excellence

**When to Use**: Reporting on healthcare quality improvement

**Latest Version**: SQUIRE 2.0 (2015)

**Major Sections (18 items):**
1. Title and Abstract
2. Introduction (Problem description, Available knowledge, Rationale, Specific aims)
3. Methods (Context, Intervention, Study design, Measures, Analysis, Ethical review)
4. Results (Intervention, Outcomes)
5. Discussion (Summary, Interpretation, Limitations, Conclusions)
6. Other Information (Funding)

**Access**: http://www.squire-statement.org/

### CHEERS - Economic Evaluations

**Full Name**: Consolidated Health Economic Evaluation Reporting Standards

**When to Use**: Health economic evaluations

**Latest Version**: CHEERS 2022 (major update from 2013)

**Major Checklist Items (28 items):**
1. Title identifying as economic evaluation
2. Abstract
3. Background and objectives
4. Target population and subgroups
5. Setting and location
6. Study perspective
7. Comparators
8. Time horizon
9. Discount rate
10. Choice of outcomes
11. Measurement of effectiveness
12. Measurement and valuation of costs
13. Currency and price adjustments
14. Model choice
15. Assumptions
16. Analytical methods

**Access**: https://www.equator-network.org/reporting-guidelines/cheers/

### SRQR - Qualitative Research

**Full Name**: Standards for Reporting Qualitative Research

**When to Use**: Qualitative and mixed-methods research

**Latest Version**: SRQR 2014

**Major Sections:**
- Title and Abstract

**Alternative**: COREQ (Consolidated criteria for REporting Qualitative research for interviews and focus groups)

**Access**: https://www.equator-network.org/reporting-guidelines/srqr/

## How to Use Reporting Guidelines

### During Research Planning

1. **Identify the relevant guideline** based on study design
2. **Review items that need planning** (e.g., randomization, blinding)
3. **Design the study** to ensure all required elements are captured
4. **Consider protocol guidelines** (e.g., SPIRIT for trials)

### During Manuscript Drafting

1. **Download the checklist** from the guideline website
2. **Address each item systematically**
3. **Indicate the location** (page/line number) of each item in the manuscript
4. **Revise the manuscript** to include missing items
5. **Use flow diagrams** where appropriate

### Before Submission

1. **Complete the formal checklist** with page numbers
2. **Review that all items** are adequately addressed
3. **Include the checklist with the submission** if required by the journal
4. **State guideline adherence** in the cover letter or methods section

### Example Checklist Entry

```
Item 7: Inclusion criteria for participants, and the settings and locations where the data were collected
Page 6, Lines 112-125: "Participants were community-dwelling adults aged 60-85 with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI), 
meeting Petersen criteria. Exclusion criteria included dementia diagnosis, major psychiatric disorders, or unstable medical conditions. 
Recruitment took place from three memory clinics in Boston between January 2022 and December 2023."
```

**Example 1**: A pilot RCT with a qualitative component
- Use CONSORT for the quantitative part

- Use PRISMA for review methods
- Use STARD considerations for included studies

## Creating Flow Diagrams

### CONSORT Flow Diagram

**Four Stages:**
1. **Enrollment**: Assessment for eligibility
2. **Allocation**: Random assignment to groups
3. **Follow-up**: Receipt of intervention, loss to follow-up
4. **Analysis**: Inclusion in analysis

**Example:**
```
Assessed for eligibility (n=250)
    ↓
Excluded (n=50)
  • Not meeting criteria (n=30)
  • Declined to participate (n=15)
  • Other reasons (n=5)
    ↓
Randomized (n=200)
    ├─────────────────┬─────────────────┐
    ↓                 ↓                 ↓
Allocated to      Allocated to      Allocated to
Intervention A    Intervention B    Control
(n=67)            (n=66)            (n=67)
    ↓                 ↓                 ↓
Lost to follow-up Lost to follow-up Lost to follow-up
(n=3)             (n=5)             (n=2)
    ↓                 ↓                 ↓
Analyzed          Analyzed          Analyzed
(n=64)            (n=61)            (n=65)
```

### PRISMA Flow Diagram

**Stages:**
1. **Identification**: From databases and register records
2. **Screening**: Records screened, excluded
3. **Inclusion**: Studies included in review and synthesis

**New in PRISMA 2020:**
- Separate tracking of database and register searches
- Tracking of duplicate removal
- Clear distinction between reports and studies

## Common Errors and How to Avoid Them

### Error 1: Not using a guideline at all

**Impact**: Missing critical information, lower chance of acceptance

**Solution**: Identify and use the appropriate guideline from the research planning stage

### Error 2: Using the guideline only after completion

**Impact**: May realize critical data was not collected or recorded

**Solution**: Review guidelines during study design and data collection

### Error 3: Incomplete checklist completion

**Impact**: Missing items remain unreported

**Solution**: Address every single checklist item systematically

### Error 4: Using outdated guidelines

**Impact**: Missing the latest improvements in reporting standards

**Solution**: Always check the official guideline website for the most recent version

### Error 5: Using the wrong guideline for the study design

**Impact**: Important design-specific elements are not reported

**Solution**: Carefully match study design with the appropriate guideline

### Error 6: Not submitting the checklist when requested

**Impact**: Editorial rejection or delays

**Solution**: Check journal submission guidelines and include the checklist

### Error 7: Generic reporting lacking specificity

**Impact**: Lack of sufficient detail for replication or appraisal

**Solution**: Provide specific, detailed information for each item

## Journal Requirements

### Many journals now require:

1. A statement of adherence to reporting guidelines in the Methods
2. Uploading the completed checklist as a supplementary file
3. Indicating page/line numbers on the checklist where items are located
4. Inclusion of flow diagrams as figures in the manuscript

### Example Methods Statement:

```
This study was reported in accordance with the Strengthening the Reporting of Observational Studies in Epidemiology (STROBE) statement. 
The completed STROBE checklist is provided as Supplementary File 1.
```

### Journals with Strict Requirements:

- PLOS journals (require checklists for specific designs)
- BMJ (requires CONSORT, PRISMA, etc.)
- The Lancet (requires adherence to statements)
- JAMA and JAMA Network journals (require checklists)
- Nature Portfolio journals (encourage guidelines)

## Venue-Specific Reporting Requirements

### Reporting Standards by Venue Type

| Venue Type | Guideline Usage | Transparency Requirements |
|-----------|--------------|---------------------------|
| **Medical Journals** | Mandatory (CONSORT, STROBE, etc.) | Checklist required at submission |
| **PLOS/BMC** | Mandatory for study type | Checklist uploaded as supplement |
| **Nature/Science** | Recommended | Emphasis on methods completeness |
| **ML Conferences** | No formal guidelines | Reproducibility details required |

### ML Conference Reporting Standards

**NeurIPS/ICML/ICLR Reproducibility Requirements:**
- **Datasets**: Name, version, access method, preprocessing
- **Code**: Availability statement; GitHub is common
- **Hyperparameters**: All settings reported (learning rate, batch size, etc.)
- **Seeds**: Random seeds for reproducibility
- **Compute Resources**: GPUs used, training time
- **Statistical Significance**: Error bars, confidence intervals, multiple runs
- **Broader Impact** statement (NeurIPS): Societal implications

**What to Include (often in Appendix):**
- Full hyperparameter settings
- Training details and convergence criteria
- Hardware specifications
- Software versions (PyTorch 2.0, etc.)
- Dataset splits and any preprocessing
- Evaluation metrics and protocols

### Execution and Evaluation

**What to Check:**
- **Medical Journals**: Checklist uploaded; adherence statement in methods; systematic completeness
- **PLOS/BMC**: Mandatory checklist for certain designs; emphasis on reproducibility
- **High Impact**: Methods sufficient for replication (checklists often not required)
- **ML Conferences**: Reproducibility checklist (NeurIPS); code access increasingly expected

**Common Issues Leading to Rejection:**
- Missing required checklist (medical journals)
- Insufficient detail in replication methods
- Missing key information (randomization, blinding, power calculation)
- No data/code availability statement when required

**Example Methods Statements:**

**Journal (STROBE):**
```
This study follows the STROBE reporting guidelines. A checklist is provided in Supplementary Material 1.
```

**ML Conference (Reproducibility):**
```
Code is available at github.com/user/project. All hyperparameters are in Appendix A. 
Training used 4×A100 GPUs (~20 hours). Seeds: {42, 123, 456}.
```

### Pre-submission Reporting Checklist

**For Clinical Trials (Medical Journals):**
- [ ] Completed CONSORT checklist with page numbers
- [ ] Trial registration number in abstract and methods
- [ ] CONSORT flow diagram included
- [ ] Statistical analysis plan described
- [ ] Adherence statement in methods

**For Observational Studies (Medical/Epidemiology):**
- [ ] Completed STROBE checklist
- [ ] Study design clearly stated
- [ ] Statistical methods detailed
- [ ] Confounding factors addressed
- [ ] Adherence statement in methods

**For Systematic Reviews:**
- [ ] Completed PRISMA checklist
- [ ] PRISMA flow diagram included
- [ ] Protocol registration (PROSPERO)
- [ ] Search strategy documented
- [ ] Risk of bias assessment included

**For ML Conference Papers:**
- [ ] All datasets named and versioned
- [ ] Code availability stated (GitHub link if available)
- [ ] Hyperparameters listed (Appendix acceptable)
- [ ] Random seeds reported
- [ ] Compute resources specified
- [ ] Error bars/confidence intervals shown
- [ ] Broader impact statement (if required)
