# Principles of Scientific Writing

## Overview

Effective scientific writing requires mastering the fundamental principles that ensure clarity, precision, and impact. Unlike creative or narrative writing, scientific writing prioritizes accuracy, conciseness, and objectivity. This guide covers the core principles that distinguish good scientific writing from poor writing and provides practical strategies for improvement.

## The Three Pillars of Scientific Writing

### 1. Clarity

**Definition**: Writing that the target audience can understand immediately without ambiguity or confusion.

**Why it matters**: Science is complex enough; unclear writing should not add to the confusion. Readers should focus on understanding the science, not deciphering the prose.

#### Strategies for Clarity

**Use precise, unambiguous language:**
```
Poor: "The drug seemed to help a fair number of patients."
Good: "The drug reduced symptoms in 68% (32/47) of patients."
```

**Define technical terms upon first use:**
```
"We measured brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), a protein involved in neuronal survival and plasticity."
```

**Maintain logical flow within and between paragraphs:**
- Each paragraph should have one main point.
- A topic sentence introduces the focus of the paragraph.
- Supporting sentences develop that focus.
- Transition sentences link paragraphs.

**Use active voice when it improves clarity:**
```
Passive (less clear): "The samples were analyzed by the researchers."
Active (clearer): "The researchers analyzed the samples."
```

However, passive voice is acceptable and often preferred in the Methods section when the action is more important than the actor:
```
"Blood samples were collected at baseline and after 6 weeks."
```

**Break down long, complex sentences:**
```
Poor: "The results of our study, which involved 200 participants recruited from three hospitals and followed for 12 months with assessments every 4 weeks using validated questionnaires, showed a significant improvement in the intervention group."

Good: "Our study involved 200 participants recruited from three hospitals. Participants were followed for 12 months and assessed every 4 weeks using validated questionnaires. The intervention group showed significant improvement."
```

**Use concrete verbs:**
```
Weak: "This study investigated depression in adolescents."
Stronger: "This study examined the factors contributing to adolescent depression."
```

#### Common Clarity Issues

**Ambiguous pronouns:**
```
Poor: "Group A received the drug, and Group B received the placebo. They showed improvement."
(Who are "they"?)

Good: "Group A received the drug, and Group B received the placebo. The drug-treated group showed improvement."
```

**Misplaced modifiers:**
```
Poor: "We measured the blood pressure of patients using an automated monitor."
(Are the patients using the monitor, or are we?)

Good: "Using an automated monitor, we measured the patients' blood pressure."
```

**Unclear referents:**
```
Poor: "Increased expression was accompanied by decreased proliferation, which was unexpected."
(What was unexpected—the decrease, the accompaniment, or both?)

Good: "Increased expression was accompanied by decreased proliferation. This inverse relationship was unexpected."
```

### 2. Conciseness

**Definition**: Expressing ideas using the fewest necessary words without sacrificing clarity or completeness.

**Why it matters**: Concise writing respects the reader's time. Every unnecessary word is a missed opportunity for clarity and impact. As the principle goes: "We value concise writing because we value time."

#### Strategies for Conciseness

**Eliminate redundant words and phrases:**

| Wordy | Concise |
|-------|---------|
| "Due to the fact that" | "Because" |
| "In order to" | "To" |
| "It is important to note that" | [Delete] |
| "A total of 50 participants" | "50 participants" |
| "Completely eliminate" | "Eliminate" |
| "Has been shown to be" | "Is" |
| "In the event that" | "If" |
| "At the present time" | "Now" or "Currently" |
| "Conduct an investigation" | "Investigate" |
| "Give consideration to" | "Consider" |

**Avoid "throat-clearing" phrases:**
```
Wordy: "It is worth noting that the results of our study suggest..."
Concise: "Our results suggest..." or "Results show..."
```

**Use strong verbs instead of verb + noun combinations:**

| Wordy | Concise |
|-------|---------|
| "Make a decision" | "Decide" |
| "Perform an analysis" | "Analyze" |
| "Conduct a study" | "Study" or "Studied" |
| "Do an evaluation" | "Evaluate" |
| "Provide information" | "Inform" |

**Eliminate unnecessary intensifiers:**
```
Wordy: "The results were very significant."
Concise: "The results were significant." (The p-value conveys the degree)
```

**Avoid repeating information unnecessarily:**
```
Redundant: "The results showed that participants who received the intervention, which was a therapeutic intervention, had better outcomes."
Concise: "The intervention group had better outcomes."
```

**Favor shorter structures:**
```
Wordy: "Despite the fact that the sample size was small..."
Concise: "Although the sample size was small..."
```

#### Acceptable vs. Unnecessary Length

**Not all long sentences are bad:**
```
This detailed sentence is fine: "We analyzed blood samples using liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) with a Waters Acquity UPLC system coupled to a Waters Xevo TQ-S mass spectrometer (Waters Corporation, Milford, MA)."

Why? Because every element is necessary information.
```

**Key Question**: Can any word be removed without losing meaning or precision? If yes, remove it.

### 3. Accuracy

**Definition**: The precise and correct representation of data, methods, and interpretations.

**Why it matters**: Scientific credibility depends on accuracy. Inaccurate reporting undermines the entire scientific enterprise.

#### Strategies for Accuracy

**Report exact values with appropriate precision:**
```
Poor: "The average was about 25."
Good: "The mean was 24.7 ± 3.2 (SD)."
```

**Match precision to measurement capability:**
```
Inappropriate: "The average age was 45.237 years" (implies false precision)
Appropriate: "The average age was 45.2 years"
```

**Use consistent terminology throughout:**
```
Inconsistent: Introduction calls it "cognitive function," Methods calls it "mental performance," Results calls it "intellectual ability."

Consistent: Use "cognitive function" throughout, or define clearly: "cognitive function (also referred to as mental performance)"
```

**Distinguish between observation and interpretation:**
```
Observation: "Mean blood pressure decreased from 145 to 132 mmHg (p=0.003)."
Interpretation: "This suggests the intervention was effective in lowering blood pressure."
```

**Be specific about uncertainty:**
```
Vague: "There might be some error in these measurements."
Specific: "Based on instrument specifications, measurements have a standard error of ±2.5 mmHg."
```

**Use correct statistical language:**
```
Incorrect: "The correlation was highly significant (p=0.03)."
Correct: "The correlation was statistically significant (p=0.03)."
(p=0.03 is not "highly" significant; that is reserved for p < 0.001)
```

**Verify all numbers:**
- Check that numbers in text match tables/figures.
- Verify that n-values sum correctly.
- Confirm percentages are calculated correctly.
- Double-check all statistics.

#### Common Accuracy Issues

**Overgeneralization:**
```
Poor: "Exercise prevents depression."
Good: "In our sample, participants randomly assigned to the exercise intervention showed fewer depressive symptoms than the control group (mean difference of 3.2 points on the BDI-II scale, 95% CI: 1.5–4.9, p < 0.001)."
```

**Unjustified causal claims:**
```
Poor (from an observational study): "Vitamin D supplementation reduces cancer risk."
Good: "In this cohort, Vitamin D levels were inversely associated with cancer incidence (HR = 0.82, 95% CI: 0.71–0.95)."
```

**Imprecise numerical descriptions:**
```
Vague: "Many participants dropped out."
Precise: "15/50 (30%) participants withdrew before study completion."
```

## Additional Key Principles

### 4. Objectivity

**Definition**: Presenting information impartially, without bias, exaggeration, or unsupported opinion.

**Strategies:**

**Present results without bias:**
```
Biased: "As expected, our superior method performed better."
Objective: "Method A showed a higher accuracy rate than Method B (87% vs. 76%, p=0.02)."
```

**Acknowledge contradictory evidence:**
```
"Our findings contrast with those of Smith et al. (2022), who reported no significant effect. This discrepancy may stem from differences in intervention intensity or population characteristics."
```

**Avoid emotional or evaluative language:**
```
Subjective: "The results were disappointing and concerning."
Objective: "The intervention did not significantly reduce symptoms (p=0.42)."
```

**Distinguish between fact and speculation:**
```
"The observed decrease in cell viability was accompanied by increased caspase-3 activity, suggesting that apoptosis may be the primary mechanism of cell death."
(Use "suggesting" and "may be" to indicate interpretation)
```

### 5. Consistency

**Maintain consistency across the manuscript:**

**Terminology:**
- Use the same term for the same concept (do not use synonyms for variety).
- Define abbreviations at first use and use them consistently thereafter.
- Use standard nomenclature for genes, proteins, and chemicals.

**Notation:**
- Statistical symbols (p-value formatting, CI representation).
- Units of measurement.
- Number formatting (decimal places).

**Tense:**
- Use past tense for your specific research actions.
- Use present tense for established facts.
- See the Tense Guide in the IMRAD structure reference for details.

**Style:**
- Follow journal guidelines consistently.
- Citation format.
- Heading capitalization.
- Numerals vs. word forms of numbers.

### 6. Logical Organization

**Create a clear "red thread" through the manuscript:**

**Paragraph Structure:**
1. Topic sentence (main idea).
2. Supporting sentences (evidence, explanation).
3. Concluding/Transition sentence (link to the next idea).

**Section Flow:**
- Each section builds logically on the previous one.
- Questions raised in the Introduction are answered in the Results.
- Findings presented in the Results are explained in the Discussion.

**Signposting:**
```
"First, we examined..."
"Next, we investigated..."
"Finally, we assessed..."
```

**Parallel Structure:**
```
Not Parallel: "The aims were (1) measuring blood pressure, (2) cognitive function assessment, and (3) we wanted to evaluate mood."

Parallel: "The aims were (1) to measure blood pressure, (2) to assess cognitive function, and (3) to evaluate mood."
```

## Verb Tenses in Scientific Writing

### General Guidelines

**Present Tense** is used for:
- Established facts and general truths.
  - "DNA is composed of nucleotides."
- Conclusions you are drawing.
  - "These findings suggest..."
- References to figures and tables.
  - "Figure 1 shows the distribution..."

**Past Tense** is used for:
- Specific findings from completed studies (yours and others).
  - "Smith et al. (2022) found..."
  - "We observed a significant decrease..."
- Methods you performed.
  - "Participants completed a questionnaire at baseline."

**Present Perfect Tense** is used for:
- Recent developments with current relevance.
  - "Recent studies have demonstrated..."
- Research area background.
  - "Several methods have been proposed..."

### Tense by Specific Section

| Section | Primary Tense | Example |
|---------|---------------|----------|
| **Abstract - Background** | Present or Present Perfect | "Depression affects millions" / "Studies have shown..." |
| **Abstract - Methods** | Past | "We recruited 100 participants" |
| **Abstract - Results** | Past | "The intervention reduced symptoms" |
| **Abstract - Conclusion** | Present | "These findings suggest..." |
| **Introduction - Background** | Present (facts), Present Perfect (research) | "Exercise is beneficial" / "Studies have shown..." |
| **Introduction - Gap** | Present or Present Perfect | "However, little is known..." |
| **Introduction - This study** | Past or Present | "We investigated..." / "This study investigates..." |
| **Methods** | Past | "We collected samples..." |
| **Results** | Past | "The mean age was 45 years" |
| **Discussion - Your findings** | Past | "We found that..." |
| **Discussion - Interpretation** | Present | "This suggests that..." |
| **Discussion - Prior work** | Past or Present | "Smith found..." / "Prior work demonstrates..." |

## Common Writing Pitfalls

### 1. Jargon Overload

**Problem**: Excessive use of technical terms without definition.

**Example:**
```
Poor: "We utilized qRT-PCR to quantify mRNA expression following cDNA synthesis from total RNA using oligo-dT primers with SYBR-Green-based fluorescence detection."

Good: "We quantified mRNA expression using quantitative reverse transcription PCR (qRT-PCR). Total RNA was reverse transcribed into complementary DNA (cDNA) using oligo-dT primers, followed by amplification with SYBR Green fluorescence detection."
```

### 2. Nominalization

**Problem**: Turning verbs into nouns, making writing heavy and indirect.

**Example:**

| Nominalization | Direct |
|-------------|--------|
| "Give consideration to" | "Consider" |
| "Make an assumption" | "Assume" |
| "Conduct an investigation" | "Investigate" |
| "Perform an examination" | "Examine" |
| "Achieve a reduction" | "Reduce" |

### 3. Over- or Under-Hedging

**Over-hedging** (sounds uncertain):
```
"The intervention might possibly have some effect on symptoms under certain conditions that could be possible."
```

**Under-hedging** (overstating conclusions):
```
"The intervention cures depression."
```

**Appropriate Hedging:**
```
"In this sample, the intervention significantly reduced depressive symptoms, suggesting it may be effective for treating mild-to-moderate depression."
```

**Appropriately used hedging words:**
- Suggest, indicate, imply (not prove, demonstrate for correlational data)
- May, might, could (possibility)
- Seem, appear (observations needing confirmation)
- Likely, probably, possibly (degree of certainty)

### 4. Anthropomorphism

**Problem**: Attributing human characteristics to non-human entities.

**Example:**

| Anthropomorphism | Scientific |
|----------------|-----------|
| "The study wanted to examine..." | "We aimed to examine..." or "The study examined..." |
| "The data suggest they want to..." | "The data suggest..." |
| "This paper will prove..." | "This paper demonstrates..." |
| "Table 1 tells us..." | "Table 1 shows..." |

### 5. Abbreviation Abuse

**Problems:**
- Burdening the reader with too many abbreviations.
- Abbreviating terms used only once or twice.
- Failing to define abbreviations at first use.

**Guidelines:**
- Only abbreviate terms used ≥3–4 times.
- Define at first use in the Abstract (if used there).
- Define at first use in the main text.
- Do not abbreviate in the title.
- Limit to 3–4 new abbreviations per paper when possible.
- Use standard abbreviations (DNA, RNA, HIV, etc.) without definition.

**Example:**
```
Poor: "We measured brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) at baseline. BDNF levels were elevated."
(Used only twice, abbreviation is unnecessary)

Good: "We measured brain-derived neurotrophic factor at baseline. Levels were elevated."
```

## Specific Sentence-Level Issues

### Dangling Modifiers

**Problem:**
```
"After incubating for 2 hours, we measured the absorbance."
(The sentence suggests "we" were incubated)

Good: "After the samples were incubated for 2 hours, we measured the absorbance."
OR: "After a 2-hour incubation, we measured the absorbance."
```

### Comma Misuse

**Common Errors:**

**Between subject and verb:**
```
Wrong: "Participants in the intervention group, showed improvement."
Right: "Participants in the intervention group showed improvement."
```

**In compound predicates:**
```
Wrong: "We measured blood pressure, and recorded symptoms."
Right: "We measured blood pressure and recorded symptoms."
(No comma needed before "and" when it doesn't link independent clauses)
```

### Pronoun Agreement

```
Wrong: "Each participant completed their questionnaire."
Right: "Each participant completed his or her questionnaire."
OR BETTER: "Participants completed their questionnaires."
```

### Subject-Verb Agreement

```
Wrong: "The group of participants were heterogeneous."
Right: "The group of participants was heterogeneous."
(The subject is "group" [singular], not "participants")

BUT: "Participants were heterogeneous." (Plural subject)
```

## Word Choice

### Commonly Confused Words in Scientific Writing

| Often Misused | Correct Usage |
|---------------|---------------|
| **Affect / Effect** | Affect (verb): to influence; Effect (noun): result; Effect (verb): to bring about |
| **Among / Between** | Among: three or more; Between: two |
| **Continual / Continuous** | Continual: repeated; Continuous: uninterrupted |
| **Data are / Data is** | Data are (plural); Data is (singular - though "are" is traditional in science) |
| **Fewer / Less** | Fewer: countable items; Less: continuous quantities |
| **i.e. / e.g.** | i.e. (id est): that is; e.g. (exempli gratia): for example |
| **Imply / Infer** | Imply: to suggest; Infer: to deduce |
| **Parameter / Variable** | Parameter: population value; Variable: measured characteristic |
| **Principal / Principle** | Principal: main; Principle: rule or concept |
| **Significant** | Reserved for statistical significance, not importance |
| **That / Which** | That: restrictive clause; Which: non-restrictive clause |

### Words to Avoid or Use Carefully

**Avoid informal language:**
- "A lot of" → "Many" or "Numerous"
- "Get" → "Obtain" or "Become"
- "Show up" → "Appear" or "Be evident"

**Avoid vague quantifiers:**
- "Some" → Specify how many
- "Often" → Specify frequency
- "Recently" → Specify time frame

**Avoid unnecessary qualifiers:**
- "Very significant" → "Significant" (p-value shows degree)
- "Quite large" → "Large" or specify size
- "Rather interesting" → Delete or explain why

## Numbers and Units

### When to Use Numerals vs. Words

**Use numerals for:**
- All numbers ≥10.
- Numbers with units (5 mg, 3 mL).
- Statistical values (p = 0.03, t = 2.14).
- Ages, dates, times.
- Scores and scales.
- Percentages (15%).

**Use words for:**
- Numbers <10 not connected to units (five participants).
- Numbers at the beginning of a sentence (spell out or restructure).

**Example:**
```
"Five participants withdrew" OR "There were 5 dropouts"
(NOT: "5 participants withdrew")

"We tested 15 samples at 3 time points"
"The average age was 45 years"
```

### Units and Formatting

**Guidelines:**
- Space between number and unit (5 mg, not 5mg).
- No period after units (mg not mg.).
- Use SI units unless field convention differs.
- Be consistent in decimal places.
- Use commas for thousands in text (12,500 not 12500).

**Ranges:**
- Use an en-dash (–) for ranges: 15–20 mg.
- Include the unit only after the second number: 15–20 mg (not 15 mg–20 mg).

## Paragraph Structure

### Ideal Paragraph Length

**Guidelines:**
- Typically 3–7 sentences.
- One main idea per paragraph.
- Too short (<2 sentences): May indicate an idea needs development or merging.
- Too long (>10 sentences): Likely needs to be split.

### Paragraph Coherence

**Techniques:**

**1. Topic Sentences:**
```
"Exercise training improves cardiovascular function through multiple mechanisms.
[Following sentences explain these mechanisms]"
```

**2. Transition Phrases:**
- First, second, third, finally.
- Furthermore, moreover, additionally.
- However, nevertheless, conversely.
- Therefore, thus, consequently.
- For example, specifically, in particular.

**3. Repetition of Key Terms:**
```
"...this mechanism of action. This mechanism may explain..."
(NOT: "...this mechanism. This process may explain...")
```

**4. Parallel Structure:**
```
"Group A received the drug. Group B received the placebo. Group C received no treatment."
(NOT: "Group A received the drug. Group B was given the placebo. No treatment was provided to the third group.")
```

## Revision Checklist

### Content Level

- [ ] Does every sentence add value?
- [ ] Are claims supported by data?
- [ ] Is the logic clear and sound?
- [ ] Are interpretations warranted by the results?

### Paragraph Level

- [ ] Does each paragraph have one main idea?
- [ ] Are paragraphs in a logical order?
- [ ] Are transitions smooth?
- [ ] Is there a clear "red thread"?

### Sentence Level

- [ ] Are sentences clear and concise?
- [ ] Is sentence structure varied?
- [ ] Are there no dangling modifiers?
- [ ] Do subjects and verbs agree?

### Word Level

- [ ] Is word choice precise?
- [ ] Are technical terms defined?
- [ ] Is terminology consistent?
- [ ] Are abbreviations necessary and defined?
- [ ] Are numbers formatted correctly?

### Grammar and Mechanics

- [ ] Are verb tenses correct and consistent?
- [ ] Are commas used correctly?
- [ ] Do pronouns agree with antecedents?
- [ ] Is punctuation correct?
- [ ] Is spelling correct (including technical terms)?

## Tools for Improving Writing

### Grammar and Style Checkers

- **Grammarly**: Grammar, style, clarity.
- **ProWritingAid**: In-depth writing analysis.
- **Hemingway Editor**: Readability, simplification.
- **LanguageTool**: Open-source grammar checker.

**Note**: These tools do not understand scientific writing conventions. Use them as a starting point, not the final arbiter.

### Readability Metrics

**Flesch Reading Ease:**
- 60–70: Acceptable for scientific papers.
- <60: Likely too complex.

**Note**: Do not sacrifice precision for readability scores designed for general audiences.

### Peer Review

**The most valuable tool:**
- Ask a colleague to read and provide feedback.
- Identify unclear passages.
- Check logical flow.
- Verify if interpretations are reasonable.

## Venue-Specific Writing Styles

### Four Major Writing Style Categories

1. **Broad Audience Accessible** (Nature, Science, PNAS)
2. **Clinical Professional** (NEJM, Lancet, JAMA)
3. **Technical Professional** (Field-specific journals)
4. **ML Conferences** (NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR, CVPR)

### Comparison of Writing Styles

| Aspect | Nature/Science | Medical | Professional | ML Conferences |
|--------|---------------|---------|-------------|---------------|
| **Sentence Length** | 15–20 words | 12–18 words | 18–25 words | 12–20 words |
| **Vocabulary** | Minimal jargon | Clinical terms | Field-specific | Technical + Math |
| **Tone** | Engaging, Significant | Conservative | Formal | Direct, Contribution-focused |
| **Key Phrases** | "We show here" | "We conducted" | "To elucidate" | "We propose", "Our contributions" |

**ML Conference Style:**

**Characteristics:**
- Direct, technical language with mathematical notation.
- Contribution-focused (numbered lists are common).
- Assumes ML expertise (CNNs, transformers, SGD, etc.).
- Emphasis on novelty and performance gains.
- Expectation of pseudocode and equations.

**Example Opening (NeurIPS style):**
```
"Vision transformers have achieved state-of-the-art performance on image classification, but their quadratic complexity limits applicability to high-resolution images. We propose Efficient-ViT, which reduces complexity to O(n log n) while maintaining accuracy. Our contributions are: (1) a new sparse attention mechanism, (2) theoretical analysis showing expressivity is preserved, and (3) empirical validation on ImageNet showing a 15% speedup with comparable accuracy."
- Problem statement with technical context.
- Preview of solution.
- Numbered contributions.
- Quantified claims.
```

### Key Writing Differences

| Aspect | Nature/Science | Medical | Professional | ML Conferences |
|--------|---------------|---------|-------------|---------------|
| **Paragraph Length** | 3–5 sentences | 5–7 sentences | 6–10 sentences | 4–6 sentences |
| **Math/Equations** | Minimized | Rare | Moderate | Essential |
| **Active Voice** | Preferred | Mixed | Passive OK | Preferred |
| **Hedging** | Moderate | Conservative | Detailed | Minimal (claim gains) |
| **Figure Integration** | Tight | Systematic | Detailed | Dense, in-page |

### Evaluation Focus by Venue

| Venue | Key Evaluation Criteria |
|-------|------------------------|
| **Nature/Science** | Accessible to non-specialists? Broad significance clear? Engaging story? |
| **Medical** | Clinical relevance obvious? Professional tone? Methods sufficient? |
| **Professional** | Technical precision? Shows field expertise? Methods detailed? |
| **ML Conferences** | Clear contributions? Experiments support claims? Reproducible? |

**Common Rejection Reasons:**
- Poor writing quality / unclear prose.
- Style not appropriate for the venue.
- Overstated claims.
- Insufficient methods for replication.
- Missing key details (baselines, ablations for ML; statistics for journals).

### Quick Style Adaptation Guide

| From → To | Key Changes |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Journal → ML Conference** | Add numbered contributions; include equations/pseudocode; emphasize quantified gains; compress prose. |
| **ML Conference → Journal** | Remove contribution numbering; expand motivation; separate results/discussion; reduce equations in main text. |
| **Professional → Broad** | Simplify language; emphasize broad significance; explain technical concepts for non-experts; add context. |
| **Broad → Professional** | Add technical detail; use field jargon freely; expand mechanism discussion; cite niche literature. |
| **Basic Science → Clinical** | Add patient/clinical context; use clinical language; emphasize outcomes/significance; cite clinical evidence. |

### Pre-Submission Style Checklist

**All Venues:**
- [ ] Writing style matches 3–5 recent papers from the venue.
- [ ] Sentence length is appropriate.
- [ ] Technical vocabulary level is correct.
- [ ] Tone is consistent with the venue.
- [ ] No overstated claims.

**ML Conference Additions:**
- [ ] Clearly numbered contributions in the Introduction.
- [ ] Mathematical notation is correct and consistent.
- [ ] Pseudocode/algorithms included where appropriate.
- [ ] Claims are quantified (e.g., "15% faster", "2.3% accuracy gain").
- [ ] Limitations are acknowledged.

## Final Thoughts

Effective scientific writing is a skill developed through practice. The key principles:

1. **Clarity** over complexity.
2. **Conciseness** respects the reader's time.
3. **Accuracy** builds credibility.
4. **Objectivity** maintains scientific integrity.
5. **Consistency** aids understanding.
6. **Logical Organization** guides the reader.
7. **Venue-Specific Adaptation** maximizes publication success.

**Remember**: The goal is not to impress the reader with vocabulary or complexity, but to communicate your science clearly and precisely so that others can understand, evaluate, and build upon your work. Tailor your writing style to match the expectations and audience of your target journal.
